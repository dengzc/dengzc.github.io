I study health and development economics. My research is inspired by the belief that expenditures in health, food and clean air represent an investment in the most valuable resource we have, the people itself. Specifically, my current work has focused on looking into the implementation, the effectiveness and the mechanism of policies which can provide those essentials. I am also interested in addressing econometric issues motivated by empirical work.

    Job market paper

    • Clearing the Smog: Monitors, Accountability and Pollution Reduction in China with Sebastian Axbard While a large literature focuses on evaluating the effects of different environmental policies, less attention has been given to the role of information in the implementation of these policies. The effectiveness of policies in developing countries is often plagued by the lack of trustworthy information. This risks undermining accountability – especially if there is scope for manipulation. We study this issue in the context of pollution prevention in China. Combining satellite data on pollution with the roll-out of a nationwide monitoring program, determined by strict assignment criteria that we exploit for identification, we show that: (1) installing an additional pollution monitor reduces actual pollution by 2-3% – an effect driven by pre-determined performance incentives, (2) the reduction in air pollution caused by an additional monitor is related to a 20% increase in enforcement practices in environmental regulations, (3) imperfect oversight of monitoring leads to manipulation of data when incentives for pollution reduction are strong and (4) such manipulation can be mitigated, and greater pollution reduction achieved, by re-assigning the control of information to an independent third party that is sufficiently insulated from government influence.
